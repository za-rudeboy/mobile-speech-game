# Language Target List

## Purpose
This list is for a custom parent-guided language support app for a 6-year-old autistic child with speech and language delays. The focus is not on naming objects he already knows well. The focus is on concepts that are useful in daily life, easier to reinforce outside the app, and likely to improve understanding and communication.

## Selection Rules
Choose targets that are:

- useful every day
- easy to show with pictures, objects, or actions
- usable across home routines and play
- low-frustration to practice
- possible to reinforce outside the app

## Recommended First 20 Targets

### Group 1: Perspective and Possession
These are high value because they show up constantly in turn-taking, requests, and shared play.

1. `my`
2. `your`
3. `mine`
4. `yours`
5. `me`
6. `you`

### Group 2: Location and Position
These are concrete, visual, and easy to practice with toys and real objects.

7. `in`
8. `on`
9. `under`
10. `next to`

### Group 3: Comparison and Description
Start with contrasts before moving to comparative and superlative forms.

11. `big`
12. `small`
13. `bigger`
14. `same`
15. `different`
16. `more`

### Group 4: Sequence and Structure
These help with routines, directions, and understanding what happens next.

17. `first`
18. `then`

### Group 5: Question Understanding
These are practical for comprehension and conversational participation.

19. `where`
20. `which`

## Suggested Teaching Order
Do not introduce all 20 at once. Start with the easiest concepts to anchor in real interactions.

### Phase 1
- `my`
- `your`
- `in`
- `on`
- `big`
- `small`

### Phase 2
- `mine`
- `yours`
- `under`
- `next to`
- `same`
- `different`

### Phase 3
- `me`
- `you`
- `more`
- `where`
- `which`
- `first`
- `then`

### Phase 4
- `bigger`

`bigger` comes later because comparison across items is usually easier once basic contrast words like `big` and `small` are stable.

## What To Delay Until Later
These are reasonable later targets, but not good first targets for the MVP.

- `biggest`
- `smallest`
- `before`
- `after`
- `his`
- `her`
- `they`
- `them`
- `least`
- `most`

## High-Value Bonus Targets
If the app expands beyond the first 20, these are strong next candidates.

- `help me`
- `not that`
- `again`
- `stop`
- `wait`
- `I don't know`
- `show me`
- `open`
- `find`
- `give`

These are especially useful because they improve communication repair, not just vocabulary.

## Parent Guidance
A target is worth keeping if it:

- appears naturally during the day
- can be practiced in play, meals, dressing, or routines
- makes it easier for him to understand directions or express himself

A target is not worth forcing if it:

- only works inside the app
- becomes a drill with no daily-life use
- creates repeated frustration

## Success Measure
A target is considered useful when he starts showing one or more of these outside the app:

- better understanding of your instruction
- more accurate choice-making
- spontaneous use during play or routine
- less frustration when clarifying what he means
